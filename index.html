Building transportation systems that connect people and strengthen communities

I'm a transportation systems graduate student at NYU Tandon with industry experience deploying autonomous vehicles and scaling robotic platforms. Why I Do This Work

Growing up as the child of Bangladeshi immigrants in a predominantly white suburb of Michigan, I experienced life between two cultures. A defining family trip to New York City opened my eyes to the transformative power of public transportation. Riding the Staten Island Ferry and navigating the subway system, I witnessed how transit brings together people of every background, language, and walk of life. That experience showed me something important: public transportation is more than infrastructure. It's a crucial connector that empowers individuals and builds community.

That conviction has guided my career. After studying mechanical engineering at the University of Michigan, I spent nearly a decade in industry working at the frontier of autonomous systems, robotics, and mobility technology. Now, as a graduate student at NYU Tandon, I'm building the quantitative and planning skills to ensure these technologies serve the people who need them most.

What I Learned in the AV Industry

At May Mobility, I led one of the first rural autonomous vehicle deployments in the United States: the goMARTI program in Grand Rapids, Minnesota. Over 24 months, we completed more than 10,000 trips with 98% rider satisfaction, serving a community with very limited mobility options. I planned routes, built data infrastructure, tuned vehicle sensors for harsh Minnesota winters, and worked directly with community leaders and transit agencies to integrate our service into existing mobility networks.

The experience was transformative. I saw firsthand that technology, when implemented thoughtfully and strategically, can create safe and accessible alternatives to car ownership or unreliable transit service. But I also saw the gaps. We struggled to optimize service during unpredictable demand peaks, had to balance coverage area against operational efficiency in areas with limited connectivity, and couldn't adapt quickly enough to shifting community needs. These were planning and systems problems, not just engineering ones.

At Formant, I continued this work from the platform side, consulting robotics companies from early-stage to enterprise scale on how to deploy and operate autonomous fleets. I saw the same pattern across industries: the technology works, but the systems thinking needed to deploy it equitably and sustainably requires a different kind of expertise.

What I'm Building Toward

I believe the next decade of transportation will be defined by how well cities integrate new mobility technologies with existing infrastructure. This means managing today's challenges better: optimizing bus networks, using data to guide bike share station placement and expansion into underserved neighborhoods, evaluating e-scooter pilot programs with rigor, and making evidence-based decisions about where to invest limited transit dollars. And it means preparing for what's coming: AV pilots, robotaxi services, and autonomous transit that need to be deployed as community resources, not premium luxuries.

I want to do this work in the public sector. The decisions that shape how a city moves belong in public hands, and the analytical tools that inform those decisions should be built by people who understand both the technical complexity and the communities being served. At its core, I develop frameworks and analyses that translate complex data into actionable decisions, so that the people making policy can make it with evidence, not intuition. That means not just running the model, but building the dashboard, writing the briefing, and telling the story that helps a non-technical decision-maker act.

Engineering Applications of Deep Learning

Predicting Traffic Incident Duration Using ML + LSTM Text Embeddings

Traffic incidents are the leading source of non-recurring congestion in the United States. When a crash happens, traffic management centers need to know how long it will take to clear — to allocate responders, reroute traffic, and update navigation systems. But predicting clearance time is notoriously difficult because it depends on severity, location, weather, and response logistics, much of which is captured only in free-text operator notes that traditional models ignore.

Why This Matters for Agencies

The Problem Agencies Face

Traffic management centers currently rely on average historical clearance times to plan response. A "Vehicle Accident" might take 15 minutes or 3 hours — but the categorical label alone can't distinguish between a fender-bender and a multi-vehicle pileup with hazmat.

This research demonstrates that operator notes already contain the signal needed for far better predictions. Agencies don't need new sensors or data collection — they need better models applied to the data they already generate.

Technical Approach

The pipeline has two stages. First, 326 structured features (temporal, incident type, location, severity, response agencies) are extracted from VDOT incident records and one-hot encoded. Second, an LSTM neural network processes the free-text operator notes — learning to map incident descriptions into a compact 64-dimensional embedding that captures severity nuance lost in categorical codes.

Key Finding

LSTM embeddings alone (64 features, R² = 0.65) slightly outperformed all 326 structured features (R² = 0.63) when tested with Ridge Regression — meaning the text descriptions contain predictive information not encoded in any categorical field. An operator writing "overturned tractor trailer blocking all lanes" tells the model far more than a label reading "Vehicle Accident."

What the Embeddings Capture

The LSTM learns to map textual patterns into a continuous vector space. Terse two-line notes signal something different from detailed paragraphs documenting multi-agency coordination. The word choices operators use likely reflect an implicit severity assessment — and the model picks up on this, learning that certain phrases or levels of detail correlate with longer durations even when we can't easily articulate what those patterns are.

Implementation Path for Agencies

Low barrier to adoption. This approach doesn't require new sensors or data collection — it works on data traffic management centers already produce. Agencies could deploy this model to provide real-time duration estimates the moment an incident is logged, improving resource allocation, traveler information accuracy, and downstream congestion management.

Future improvements: integrating weather data, exploring BERT-based embeddings for richer text representations, and building interpretability tools so managers understand which phrases drive predictions.

Forecasting Urban Travel Demand

From Uber to Autonomous Ride-Hailing — Analyzing NYC Transportation Disruption

As autonomous vehicles move from pilot programs to commercial deployment, transportation planners need empirical frameworks for anticipating how this next disruption will reshape urban systems. This study uses the TNC (Uber/Lyft) disruption as a natural experiment — tracking how ride-hailing reshaped trip generation and mode choice in New York City from 2009 to 2022 to project what robotaxis might do next.

Why This Matters for Cities

The Planning Challenge

Cities are making billion-dollar infrastructure decisions today based on travel demand forecasts that may not account for how autonomous mobility services will reshape behavior. Should transit agencies invest in more fixed-route capacity, or pivot toward flexible last-mile connections? Will robotaxis cannibalize transit ridership or feed it?

This research provides empirical evidence from the TNC disruption — the closest historical analogue to what's coming — showing that income barriers fall fast through service-based models, age barriers persist, and transit faces selective vulnerability depending on trip purpose.

Two-Part Methodology

Trip Generation used linear regression on NHTS survey data (2009, 2017, 2022) filtered to the NYC metro area, predicting household trip counts from household size, income, vehicle ownership, workers, drivers, population density, and life cycle stage. The NHTS data required structured querying across codebook fields (CDIVMSAR, URBAN, MSASIZE) to isolate Mid-Atlantic urban responses with heavy rail access — essentially SQL-style filtering across a relational survey database of 4,800+ household records per year. By comparing coefficients across survey years, the study tracked how ride-share changed the factors driving trip-making.

Mode Choice used multinomial logit models on NYC Citywide Mobility Survey data (2017, 2019, 2022) with six alternatives (auto, transit, taxi, TNC, walk, bike) and three explanatory variables (income, trip distance, age). Trip-level records were merged with household and person demographic tables to construct analysis datasets — a data pipeline requiring careful joins, missing-value handling, and logical consistency checks across 5,000 observations per model. The visualization pipeline (7 publication-quality chart types) was built in matplotlib/seaborn to translate coefficient evolution into clear narratives for non-technical audiences.

Key Finding — Age Barriers Persist

Unlike income effects which democratized rapidly, TNC age coefficients remained negative and actually strengthened over time (β = −0.33 → −0.70 for non-work trips). Older travelers' resistance reflects fundamental preferences for human-operated services, not unfamiliarity that exposure resolves. This has direct implications: the populations most cited as potential AV beneficiaries (elderly non-drivers) express the most resistance to driverless technology.

Implications for Bike Share & Micromobility

The model includes bike as one of six mode alternatives, and the results carry direct relevance for shared micromobility planning. Bike ASCs declined sharply (non-work: -0.93 → -3.75; work: +0.07 → -3.17), suggesting cycling preference eroded as TNCs expanded, particularly for work trips. Distance coefficients for bike remained strongly negative across all years, confirming that physical exertion limits practical range. Combined with the age sensitivity patterns, these findings inform station density decisions: bike share expansion into new neighborhoods should account for the demographic profiles that predict adoption (younger, shorter-trip corridors) while developing targeted strategies for populations where cycling competes less effectively against TNCs and auto.

Recommendations for Policymakers & Shared Mobility Programs

Shared mobility income barriers fall fast through service-based access. Agencies managing bike share and e-scooter programs should expect rapid cross-income adoption once service areas expand, rather than assuming these remain niche or premium services.

Transit faces selective vulnerability — work commuting is under pressure (ASC declined 1.6 units), but non-work transit shows resilience. Agencies should focus improvements on the commute market and explore shared mobility partnerships (bike share as first/last-mile) rather than viewing them as competition.

Age and distance patterns should guide station siting. Bike and e-scooter preference drops sharply with age and trip distance. Expansion planning should use demographic and trip-length data to identify high-adoption corridors and pair station density with the populations most likely to ride.

Hybrid deployment strategies with human presence (e.g., one operator monitoring multiple vehicles) can bridge the age acceptance gap for autonomous services — 66% acceptance with a human onboard vs. just 13% without.

MTA B43 Route Analysis — Stop & Schedule Optimization

New York City's bus network serves 1.1 million daily riders — disproportionately lower-income residents and people with disabilities in outer-borough transit deserts. Yet ridership has been in freefall for two decades, dropping from 2 million daily in 2002 to roughly half today. The B43, connecting Greenpoint to Lefferts Gardens across 6.5 miles of Brooklyn, averages just 6.5 MPH — slower than cycling and barely faster than a brisk walk — with stops spaced only ~200m apart versus the 400–800m industry recommendation.

Why This Matters for Transit Agencies

The Opportunity

Stop consolidation is a low-cost, high-impact lever that agencies can deploy without new legislation, capital construction, or interagency coordination. The B43 already underwent consolidation in MTA's Brooklyn network redesign, yet performance remains poor — this analysis shows further optimization is both possible and quantifiable.

The methodology is directly transferable to any route in any city. The two-stage framework (stop optimization → schedule optimization) captures the linkage between route-level decisions and system-level outcomes that single-stage approaches miss.

Two-Stage Optimization Framework

Stage 1 — Stop Consolidation uses stochastic binary integer programming (via Gurobi) to determine which stops to retain. The objective minimizes total passenger cost: in-vehicle time cost for all riders plus walking penalty costs for displaced riders at removed stops. Ten ridership scenarios were generated using triangular distributions (±25% of base) to ensure the solution is robust to demand uncertainty.

Stage 2 — Vehicle Scheduling applies minimum cost network flow to assign buses to trips given the reduced cycle times. For each pair of trips, the model evaluates whether a single bus can serve both in sequence, then finds the minimum-cost assignment covering all trips.

Data infrastructure: The analysis integrates GTFS transit data (a relational database of stops, trips, stop_times, and routes tables joined via foreign keys), NYCDOT pedestrian counts, Census demographic data, and MATSim agent-based simulation outputs. Ridership was distributed across hourly intervals using MTA system-wide patterns, and stop-level boardings were estimated by scaling simulation output to match MTA reported averages. All data processing used Python (pandas, geopandas) with SQL-style filtering, merging, and aggregation across multiple source tables.

Key Finding — Non-Linear Relationship

The relationship between travel time reduction and fleet requirements is non-linear and depends on demand patterns. Weekday/Saturday schedules are "fleet-constrained" by peak-hour pinch points — even with faster trips, you still need the same number of simultaneous buses during rush hour. Sunday's flatter demand profile allowed the time savings to actually eliminate a bus. This means stop consolidation delivers its greatest fleet benefits during off-peak periods.

Recommendations & Transferability

Hybrid implementation strategy: Reinvest weekday/Saturday savings into ~3% more trips (higher ridership periods) while capturing Sunday fleet reduction for cost savings. This balances fiscal responsibility with ridership growth objectives.

Force multiplier: Stop consolidation amplifies the benefits of dedicated bus lanes, transit signal priority, and all-door boarding (OMNY). Each intervention makes the others more effective — coordinated deployment yields benefits greater than the sum of parts.

The methodology scales beyond buses. The core optimization framework — balancing access (walking penalty for displaced users) against system efficiency (travel time for all users) — applies to any station-siting or network density problem. Bike share station planning faces the same fundamental tradeoff: too many docks are expensive to maintain and rebalance, too few means riders walk too far. The walking penalty cost function, ridership-weighted objective, and stochastic scenario generation developed here could be adapted to optimize Citi Bike station density, evaluate e-scooter corral placement, or assess the impact of adding or removing stations during service area expansion.

Optimizing NYC's E-Bike Fleet: A Data-Driven Approach to Citi Bike Pricing & Allocation

New York City's Citi Bike system offers riders a choice between traditional pedal bikes and electric-assist e-bikes, but the two aren't interchangeable from a business or user perspective. E-bikes cost more to operate and carry a usage surcharge, yet demand for them continues to grow. This raises a practical question for system operators: how should Citi Bike price e-bikes and allocate them across stations to maximize revenue while still serving riders well?

Why This Matters for Bike Share Operations

The Operator's Dilemma

Citi Bike is in the latter stages of a five-year expansion that will put more than 40,000 bicycles within a five-minute walk of half of NYC residents. As e-bike demand grows, operators face a fleet mix question that has real revenue and equity implications: price e-bikes too high and you push lower-income riders back to pedal bikes (or off the system entirely); price too low and you can't cover the higher operating costs. Getting this right requires understanding rider behavior at a granular level — not averages, but how specific rider segments respond to price, distance, terrain, and weather.

This project builds exactly that understanding: a behavioral model estimated from real trip data that can simulate the revenue and ridership impacts of different pricing and allocation policies before they're implemented.

Methodology

Data Collection & Cleaning: Using the Citi Bike GBFS (General Bikeshare Feed Specification) API and published trip histories, I'm constructing a dataset of trips where riders had genuine access to both bike types at their origin station, filtering out cases where availability constraints made the "choice" meaningless. This revealed-preference approach ensures the model captures actual behavioral decisions, not artifacts of bike availability.

Discrete Choice Model: A binary logit model quantifies how factors like trip distance, elevation change, weather conditions, time of day, and price influence a rider's decision to pick an e-bike over a pedal bike. The model is estimated using maximum likelihood on the cleaned dataset, producing coefficients that can be interpreted as willingness-to-pay and price elasticity measures.

Monte Carlo Simulation: The estimated model feeds into a simulation framework that tests different pricing and fleet allocation scenarios under realistic uncertainty (variable weather, fluctuating demand, shifting station-level availability). Rather than producing a single point estimate, the simulation generates outcome distributions with confidence intervals, enabling statistically grounded comparisons across policy alternatives.

Analytical Framework: The project is grounded in the Manheim-Florian-Gaudry (MFG) framework for transportation systems analysis, connecting activity systems, transport system design, demand modeling, and performance evaluation into a unified structure.

Which rider segments are most price-sensitive. Where e-bikes add the most value relative to pedal bikes. And what pricing or fleet mix changes would improve system performance without eroding ridership or rider welfare. Instead of relying on system-wide averages, operators get segment-level behavioral insights with uncertainty quantification, letting them compare policy options with statistical rigor rather than intuition.

Direct Relevance to NYC DOT Bike Share Operations

This is the exact analytical workflow a bike share program team needs: collecting real-time system data via API, cleaning and structuring it for analysis, building behavioral models that explain rider decisions, and simulating policy scenarios to compare outcomes before implementation.

The GBFS API integration demonstrates proficiency with the data infrastructure that powers Citi Bike operations, and the choice modeling framework extends naturally to questions about station siting, rebalancing strategies, and service area expansion decisions.

The equity dimension is built into the methodology: by measuring consumer surplus and price elasticity by rider segment, the analysis can flag when a pricing change would disproportionately affect lower-income riders or reduce access in underserved neighborhoods.
