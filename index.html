Transportation shapes access, and access shapes everything.

Every transportation investment is an economic decision, an equity decision, and a political decision. I build the analysis that helps public agencies make better ones. I'm a graduate student at NYU Tandon focused on the mobility options that don't require owning a car: transit, bike share, micromobility, ride-hail, and shared autonomous services. Before grad school, I spent nearly a decade deploying autonomous vehicles and scaling robotic fleets. Now I'm applying that background to expanding access for people and communities where car ownership is a barrier, not a given.

Why I Do This Work

I grew up as the child of Bangladeshi immigrants in a mostly white suburb of Michigan, constantly navigating between two cultures. On a family trip to New York City, something clicked. I saw people of every ethnicity and language sharing the same subway car, the same bus, the same ferry, treating that kind of diversity as totally normal. The whole city ran on public transit, and it connected neighborhoods in a way that made the city feel like one place instead of a thousand separate ones. That trip changed how I thought about transportation. When people can get where they need to go without a car, communities open up. People get to jobs, to doctors, to each other. Neighborhoods support each other instead of being walled off by highway infrastructure and parking lots.

After studying mechanical engineering at Michigan, I spent about eight years working in autonomous systems, robotics, and mobility tech. Now I'm back in school at NYU Tandon, learning the fundamentals of transportation planning and applying my technical background to something I care about: building transit systems that actually help communities.

Predicting Traffic Incident Duration Using ML + LSTM Text Embeddings

Traffic incidents are the leading source of non-recurring congestion in the United States. When a crash happens, traffic management centers need to know how long it will take to clear, to allocate responders, reroute traffic, and update navigation systems. But predicting clearance time is notoriously difficult because it depends on severity, location, weather, and response logistics, much of which is captured only in free-text operator notes that traditional models ignore.

Why This Matters for Agencies

The Problem Agencies Face

Traffic management centers currently rely on average historical clearance times to plan response. A "Vehicle Accident" might take 15 minutes or 3 hours, but the categorical label alone can't distinguish between a fender-bender and a multi-vehicle pileup with hazmat.

This research demonstrates that operator notes already contain the signal needed for far better predictions. Agencies don't need new sensors or data collection. They need better models applied to the data they already generate.

Technical Approach

The pipeline has two stages. First, 326 structured features (temporal, incident type, location, severity, response agencies) are extracted from VDOT incident records and one-hot encoded. Second, an LSTM neural network processes the free-text operator notes, learning to map incident descriptions into a compact 64-dimensional embedding that captures severity nuance lost in categorical codes.

Key Finding

LSTM embeddings alone (64 features, R² = 0.65) slightly outperformed all 326 structured features (R² = 0.63) when tested with Ridge Regression, meaning the text descriptions contain predictive information not encoded in any categorical field. An operator writing "overturned tractor trailer blocking all lanes" tells the model far more than a label reading "Vehicle Accident."

What the Embeddings Capture

The LSTM learns to map textual patterns into a continuous vector space. Terse two-line notes signal something different from detailed paragraphs documenting multi-agency coordination. The word choices operators use likely reflect an implicit severity assessment, and the model picks up on this, learning that certain phrases or levels of detail correlate with longer durations even when we can't easily articulate what those patterns are.

Implementation Path for Agencies

Low barrier to adoption. This approach doesn't require new sensors or data collection. It works on data traffic management centers already produce. Agencies could deploy this model to provide real-time duration estimates the moment an incident is logged, improving resource allocation, traveler information accuracy, and downstream congestion management.

Future improvements: integrating weather data, exploring BERT-based embeddings for richer text representations, and building interpretability tools so managers understand which phrases drive predictions.

Forecasting Urban Travel Demand

From Uber to Autonomous Ride-Hailing: Analyzing NYC Transportation Disruption

As autonomous vehicles move from pilot programs to commercial deployment, transportation planners need empirical frameworks for anticipating how this next disruption will reshape urban systems. This study uses the TNC (Uber/Lyft) disruption as a natural experiment, tracking how ride-hailing reshaped trip generation and mode choice in New York City from 2009 to 2022 to project what robotaxis might do next.

Why This Matters for Cities

The Planning Challenge

Cities are making billion-dollar infrastructure decisions today based on travel demand forecasts that may not account for how autonomous mobility services will reshape behavior. Should transit agencies invest in more fixed-route capacity, or pivot toward flexible last-mile connections? Will robotaxis cannibalize transit ridership or feed it?

This research provides empirical evidence from the TNC disruption, the closest historical analogue to what's coming, showing that income barriers fall fast through service-based models, age barriers persist, and transit faces selective vulnerability depending on trip purpose.

Trip Generation used linear regression on NHTS survey data (2009, 2017, 2022) filtered to the NYC metro area, predicting household trip counts from household size, income, vehicle ownership, workers, drivers, population density, and life cycle stage. The NHTS data required structured querying across codebook fields (CDIVMSAR, URBAN, MSASIZE) to isolate Mid-Atlantic urban responses with heavy rail access, essentially SQL-style filtering across a relational survey database of up to 4,900 household records per year (though the 2022 wave was substantially smaller at 439 responses). By comparing coefficients across survey years, the study tracked how ride-share changed the factors driving trip-making.

Mode Choice used multinomial logit models on NYC Citywide Mobility Survey data (2017, 2019, 2022) with six alternatives (auto, transit, taxi, TNC, walk, bike) and three explanatory variables (income, trip distance, age). Trip-level records were merged with household and person demographic tables to construct analysis datasets, a data pipeline requiring careful joins, missing-value handling, and logical consistency checks across 5,000 observations per model. The visualization pipeline (7 publication-quality chart types) was built in matplotlib/seaborn to translate coefficient evolution into clear narratives for non-technical audiences.

The income coefficient for TNC work trips dropped from β = 0.93 in 2017 to β = 0.05 in 2022, a 95% reduction in just five years. Key Finding: Age Barriers Persist

Unlike income effects which democratized rapidly, TNC age coefficients remained negative and actually strengthened over the study period, peaking at β = −0.70 in 2019 for non-work trips (up from β = −0.33 in 2017). Even after moderating slightly to β = −0.38 by 2022, the effect remained substantially stronger than its 2017 baseline. Older travelers' resistance reflects fundamental preferences for human-operated services, not unfamiliarity that exposure resolves. This has direct implications: the populations most cited as potential AV beneficiaries (elderly non-drivers) express the most resistance to driverless technology.

Implications for Bike Share & Micromobility

The model includes bike as one of six mode alternatives, and the results carry direct relevance for shared micromobility planning. Bike ASCs declined sharply (non-work: -0.93 → -3.75; work: +0.07 → -3.17), suggesting cycling preference eroded as TNCs expanded, particularly for work trips. Distance coefficients for bike remained strongly negative across all years, confirming that physical exertion limits practical range. Combined with the age sensitivity patterns, these findings inform station density decisions: bike share expansion into new neighborhoods should account for the demographic profiles that predict adoption (younger, shorter-trip corridors) while developing targeted strategies for populations where cycling competes less effectively against TNCs and auto.

Recommendations for Policymakers & Shared Mobility Programs

Shared mobility income barriers fall fast through service-based access. Agencies managing bike share and e-scooter programs should expect rapid cross-income adoption once service areas expand, rather than assuming these remain niche or premium services.

Transit faces selective vulnerability. Work commuting is under pressure (ASC declined 1.6 units), while non-work transit shows resilience. Agencies should focus improvements on the commute market and explore shared mobility partnerships (bike share as first/last-mile) as complements rather than competitors.

Age and distance patterns should guide station siting. Bike and e-scooter preference drops sharply with age and trip distance. Expansion planning should use demographic and trip-length data to identify high-adoption corridors and pair station density with the populations most likely to ride.

Hybrid deployment strategies with human presence (e.g., one operator monitoring multiple vehicles) can bridge the age acceptance gap for autonomous services, 66% acceptance with a human onboard vs. just 13% without.

MTA B43 Route Analysis: Stop & Schedule Optimization

NYC buses carry 1.1 million riders a day, mostly in outer-borough neighborhoods where the subway doesn't reach. A lot of those riders are lower-income, or have disabilities, or both. And yet ridership has been dropping for twenty years straight, from over 2 million daily in 2002 to about half that now. The B43, which runs 6.5 miles through Brooklyn from Greenpoint to Lefferts Gardens, is a good example of why. It averages 6.5 MPH. That's slower than biking and barely faster than walking. Stops are spaced about 200 meters apart, when the industry recommendation is 400 to 800 meters.

Why This Matters for Transit Agencies

The Opportunity

Stop consolidation is one of the few things a transit agency can do that doesn't require new legislation, capital construction, or coordination with other agencies. The B43 already went through consolidation as part of MTA's Brooklyn network redesign, and it's still slow. This analysis shows there's more room to improve, and we can put a dollar figure on it.

The approach works on any route. The two-stage setup (figure out which stops to cut, then re-optimize the bus schedule around the shorter travel times) captures something that single-stage methods miss: how route-level changes ripple into fleet and scheduling outcomes.

Two-Stage Optimization Framework

Stage 1: Stop Consolidation uses stochastic binary integer programming (via Gurobi) to determine which stops to retain. The objective minimizes total passenger cost: in-vehicle time cost for all riders plus walking penalty costs for displaced riders at removed stops. Ten ridership scenarios were generated using triangular distributions (±25% of base) to ensure the solution is robust to demand uncertainty.

Stage 2: Vehicle Scheduling applies minimum cost network flow to assign buses to trips given the reduced cycle times. For each pair of trips, the model evaluates whether a single bus can serve both in sequence, then finds the minimum-cost assignment covering all trips.

Data infrastructure: The analysis integrates GTFS transit data (a relational database of stops, trips, stop_times, and routes tables joined via foreign keys) and MATSim agent-based simulation outputs for stop-level ridership estimation. Ridership was distributed across hourly intervals using MTA system-wide patterns, and stop-level boardings were estimated by scaling simulation output to match MTA reported weekday averages (5,439 riders). All data processing used Python (pandas, geopandas) with SQL-style filtering, merging, and aggregation across multiple source tables.

The relationship between travel time reduction and fleet requirements is non-linear and depends on demand patterns. Weekday/Saturday schedules are "fleet-constrained" by peak-hour pinch points. Recommendations & Transferability

Mixed strategy: On weekdays and Saturdays, keep the same fleet size but use the faster cycle times to run about 3% more trips. On Sundays, when demand is flat enough that you can actually cut a bus, take the savings. This balances growing ridership against keeping costs down.

These improvements stack. Stop consolidation makes bus lanes more effective. Transit signal priority gives bigger speed gains when buses are making fewer stops. All-door boarding (once OMNY proof-of-payment rolls out fully) cuts dwell time at the stops you keep. Each of these interventions makes the others work better. Doing them together gets you more than doing them one at a time.

This same framework works beyond buses. The core tradeoff here is access versus efficiency: removing a stop saves time for everyone on the bus but makes some people walk farther. Bike share station planning has the exact same tradeoff. Too many docks are expensive to maintain and rebalance; too few and riders walk too far to find a bike. The walking penalty function, the ridership-weighted optimization, and the stochastic scenario testing from this project could all be adapted for Citi Bike station density planning, e-scooter corral placement, or evaluating what happens when you add or remove stations during an expansion.

Optimizing Citi Bike's E-Bike Fleet: A Behavioral Choice Model for Pricing & Allocation

New York City's Citi Bike system surpassed 45 million trips in 2024 across approximately 37,000 bicycles and 2,200 stations. The defining trend of the system's recent growth has been the rapid adoption of electric bikes: in 2024, e-bikes accounted for 66% of all trips despite making up only 40% of the fleet. Citi Bike set a single-day record of over 206,000 rides in September 2025, with 71% taken on e-bikes. This asymmetry between fleet composition and ridership share creates a fundamental operational challenge.

Why This Matters Now

The Operator's Dilemma

E-bikes are significantly more expensive to deploy and maintain than pedal bikes. Each requires battery swapping by staff driving vans across the city, contributing vehicle miles traveled and operational cost. NYC DOT and Lyft have begun deploying on-site charging stations, but the capital cost is substantial. Meanwhile, unmet e-bike demand (riders arriving at a station wanting an e-bike but finding none) represents both lost trips and degraded rider experience.

The timing is critical. In November 2025, the NYC Independent Budget Office released a detailed analysis of Citi Bike's pricing and costs. NYC DOT's contract with Lyft is under review, and decisions about fleet investment, pricing tiers, and expansion strategy are actively being negotiated. Research that quantifies rider preferences and estimates price sensitivity directly informs these decisions.

Policy audience: NYC DOT Bike Share Program, Lyft/Citi Bike operations and pricing team, NYC Independent Budget Office, urban micromobility researchers.

Analytical Framework (MFG)

The project is structured using the Manheim-Florian-Gaudry framework for transportation systems analysis, connecting activity systems (rider characteristics, membership type, trip purpose), transport system design (station network, fleet mix, pricing), demand modeling (binary logit choice), and performance evaluation (revenue, ridership, consumer surplus) into a unified structure with inventory-driven feedback loops.

Behavioral Choice Model

The core of the project is a binary logit model of the individual rider's choice between e-bike and pedal bike at the moment they arrive at a station. This is a revealed-preference setting: every trip in the Citi Bike data records which bike type was used, the origin/destination stations, timestamps, and membership type. The estimation sample is filtered to trips where both bike types were available at the origin station, ensuring observed choices were unconstrained.

The utility specification for the e-bike alternative includes trip distance, elevation change between OD stations, temperature, precipitation, peak-hour indicator, member/casual indicator, price differential, and e-bike availability ratio. Pedal bike is normalized as the reference alternative. The model is estimated via maximum likelihood on individual trip records.

Monte Carlo Simulation

The estimated model feeds into a simulation of full-day Citi Bike operations. For each simulated day: stations are initialized with a fleet mix; hourly arrivals are drawn from Poisson distributions calibrated to historical demand; weather is drawn from empirical seasonal distributions; each rider makes a bike-type choice using the logit model given current station availability; inventory is updated after each checkout and return. The simulation runs N = 1,000+ days per scenario to produce sampling distributions with confidence intervals.

What This Tells Decision-Makers

The price elasticity estimate answers the question NYC DOT and Lyft are actually asking right now: if we change the e-bike surcharge by X cents, how many riders do we lose? The net operating surplus tells them whether e-bikes are worth the extra cost. And consumer surplus makes sure the analysis accounts for what happens to riders, especially lower-income and casual users who are most price-sensitive, when you change the pricing.

Data Architecture

The estimation dataset is constructed by merging Citi Bike trip records (bike type, OD stations, timestamps, member/casual) with GBFS station status snapshots (real-time dock-level availability by bike type, polled at 5-10 minute intervals), NOAA hourly weather data (temperature, precipitation, wind speed), and NYC elevation data for computing elevation change between OD pairs. The critical data gap is historical station-level availability by bike type, which the GBFS feed provides in real-time but does not archive. A Python-based polling script is actively collecting and storing these snapshots in a local database.

Direct Relevance to NYC DOT Bike Share Operations

How I'm building this: I'm polling the Citi Bike GBFS station status feed every few minutes with a Python script and storing snapshots in a local database. That gets merged with trip records, NOAA weather data, and station elevation profiles to construct the estimation dataset. The whole pipeline, from API ingestion through data cleaning to model estimation, is something I've built from scratch and continue to run.

Why it matters right now: NYC DOT's contract with Lyft is under review, and the November 2025 IBO report raised specific questions about Citi Bike's pricing structure and operating costs. The price elasticity and fleet mix results from this project are aimed at exactly those questions: what happens to ridership and revenue if you change the e-bike surcharge, and whether the current fleet split is the right one.
